NVSpeech: Word-level Annotated Speech Dataset with Non-verbal Vocalization for Human-like Synthesis

Paper Github Dataset

Abstract

Non-verbal vocalizations (NVVs)—such as laughter, breathing, and interjections like “uhm” and “oh”—are essential components of natural spoken communication. They enhance the realism, expressivity, and social appropriateness of speech, yet remain largely overlooked in conventional ASR and TTS systems. To bridge this gap, we introduce NVSpeech, the largest word-level annotated dataset to date that explicitly captures both lexical content and 18 categories of para-linguistic expressions, collected entirely from real-world, human-spoken audio.

The construction and application of NVSpeech proceed in three stages:

  1. We manually annotate 48,430 utterances with word-level aligned NVV tags, covering diverse speech scenarios with high inter-annotator consistency.
  2. Using this subset, we develop the first paralinguistic-aware ASR model, which extends conventional ASR by incorporating inline NVV markers (e.g., “You’re so funny<laughter>”), treated as special decoding tokens. This model is then used to automatically label a large-scale speech corpus, producing the full NVSpeech dataset comprising 265,888 utterances and 775 hours of aligned audio.
  3. To validate the effectiveness of NVSpeech, we fine-tune a zero-shot TTS model on both human- and auto-labeled data. The model enables explicit NVV insertion at arbitrary token positions, achieving controllable and human-like synthesis beyond traditional TTS capabilities.

By unifying recognition and generation of para-linguistic cues, NVSpeech provides the first scalable foundation for expressive speech modeling at both lexical and non-verbal levels.

Figure 1. The gap between conventional speech processing systems and NVV-aware modeling. (a) Common speech datasets omit non-verbal vocalizations (NVVs), while NVSpeech provides explicit word-level NVV annotations. (b) Conventional ASR ignores NVVs; our NVV-aware ASR transcribes both lexical and paralinguistic content. (c) Standard TTS generates only text, while our model enables controllable NVV synthesis for more human-like speech.
Figure 2. Overview of our paralinguistic-aware speech recognition and generation pipeline. (1) A word-level human annotated dataset of verbal and non-verbal vocalizations is first constructed. (2) A paralinguistic-aware ASR model is trained to jointly transcribe verbal and non-verbal content. (3) This model is used to automatically annotate large-scale unlabeled speech. (4) The expanded dataset enables training a controllable and expressive TTS system that explicitly renders paralinguistic cues.
